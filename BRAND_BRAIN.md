## 3. BRAND VOICE & TONE

### The voice in one sentence:
**Experienced. Direct. Quietly confident. Zero hype.**

### Characteristics:

**Authoritative without being arrogant.**
We have done this longer than most trekkers have been alive. We don't need to sell it with exclamation marks.

**Sparse. Every word earns its place.**
If you can cut a word and the sentence still stands, cut it. We do not pad.

**Field-honest.**
We talk about altitude sickness, fitness vetting, turning back. We do not hide the hard parts. The mountain is not a theme park.

**Respect over hype.**
The Himalaya existed before us and will outlast us. Our job is to help people meet it honestly. Never use language that frames a trek as "conquering" or "crushing" a mountain.

**Operational precision.**
We cite specifics: SpO2 readings, guide-to-trekker ratios, permit success rates, years of experience. Numbers carry more weight than adjectives.

### Tone by context:

| Context | Tone |
|---|---|
| Instagram captions | Short. Declarative. Atmospheric. Like a field note. |
| Trek descriptions | Precise, sensory, honest about difficulty |
| Safety messaging | Matter-of-fact. Clinical even. No alarm, no reassurance theatre. |
| Founder/team content | Personal but not sentimental. Earned pride, not ego. |
| CTA copy | Direct. One instruction. No "click here to learn more." |
| Testimonials / social proof | Let trekkers speak. Minimal editorial framing. |

### Words we use:
- Expedition, route, summit, ascent, acclimatisation, elevation, terrain, altitude
- Protocol, certified, vetted, monitored, assessed
- Departure, batch, lead guide, trek lead
- Kinnaur, Himalaya, Everest, Annapurna, Ladakh, EBC
- Field, dispatch, base camp, trail

### Words we avoid:
- "Adventure of a lifetime" (cliché)
- "Bucket list" (commodifies the experience)
- "Conquer" / "crush" / "beast mode" (wrong relationship with the mountain)
- "Luxury" (we are not luxury — we are precise and safe)
- "Affordable" / "cheapest" (we do not compete on price)
- "Epic" / "insane" / "mind-blowing" (tourist-brochure language)
- Excessive exclamation marks

## 4. VISUAL IDENTITY

### Palette:
- **Primary accent:** Burnt orange / coral — `#F4632E` (used sparingly for labels, CTAs, highlights)
- **Background base:** White and near-white (`slate-50`)
- **Text hierarchy:** Near-black (`slate-900`) for headlines, medium grey (`slate-500/600`) for body
- **Border / structural:** Light zinc (`zinc-border`) — grid lines, dividers
- **Dark sections:** Deep slate (`slate-900`) for hero end-sections and CTA blocks

### Photography style:
- **Greyscale by default** — photos load in greyscale, reveal colour on hover. This is intentional: it creates the sense that the mountain is revealed, not presented.
- **High contrast, high detail** — `brightness-90 contrast-110` treatment. Gritty, not polished.
- **Subject matter:** Real terrain, real guides, real trekkers mid-effort. Not golden-hour lifestyle shots with perfect hair. Snow, rock, altitude, effort.
- **No stock photography feel.** Every image should look like it was taken by someone who was actually there.

### Typography:
- Headlines: All-caps, ultra-black weight (`font-black`), extremely tight tracking (`tracking-tighter`). Monumental. Like the mountains they represent.
- Body: Small, airy, slate-grey. Designed to be read at pace — not skimmed.
- Labels / tags: Extra-small, uppercase, wide tracking (`tracking-[0.3em]`). These are wayfinding elements, not decoration.

### Layout principles:
- **Grid-based with hard borders.** No soft shadows, no rounded corners (or very minimal). The design is architectural.
- **50/50 splits.** Text on the left, image on the right. This is our primary compositional unit.
- **Whitespace is earned.** We do not fill space. Empty space signals confidence.
- **Data blocks.** Stats displayed large and raw — `847 Expeditions Led`, `6,200+ Trekkers Guided`, `16 YRS Zero Fatalities`. Let numbers speak.

## 5. OUR AUDIENCE

### Primary: The serious recreational trekker
- Age: 28–55
- Has done a trek or two before, wants to go higher
- Researches thoroughly — reads every detail, checks credentials
- Wants to know who their guide is, not just the itinerary
- Values competence over personality
- Will pay more for genuine expertise

### Secondary: The corporate / group organiser
- Planning a team expedition or milestone event
- Needs reliability and operational certainty
- Has accountability to the group — safety is non-negotiable

### Tertiary: The aspiring first-time Himalayan trekker
- Intimidated, wants reassurance delivered through facts not fluff
- Needs education more than inspiration
- Responds to: fitness requirements, SpO2 protocol, what's actually hard

### Audience pain points we address:
1. "I don't know if my guide is actually qualified or just someone with a booking account"
2. "I've seen horror stories about groups being left behind or altitude emergencies mismanaged"
3. "Every company says they're the best — I can't tell who to trust"
4. "I want a small group but every company packs 15 people in"

## 10. SOCIAL MEDIA SPECIFIC GUIDANCE

### Instagram
- Caption style: 1–3 lines max for the opening hook. No emojis in the first line.
- Use the field-note format: location, observation, implication. No fluff.
- Example format:
  > *28°N / 86°E. 5,364 metres. The air here has 50% of the oxygen at sea level.*
  > *SpO2 drops. The body adapts — or it doesn't.*
  > *We monitor. Twice a day. Every day.*
- Hashtags: use in first comment, not caption body
- Relevant hashtags: #YetiExpeditions #HimalayanTrekking #EverestBaseCamp #IFMGA #AltitudeMedicine #TrekIndia #TrekNepal #Kinnaur #HighAltitude #SpO2 #TrekkingGuide #Himalayas #AnnapurnaCircuit #EBC

### Content formats that work for us:
- **Data cards:** Large stat + small context line. E.g. "16 YEARS. ZERO FATALITIES."
- **Guide spotlights:** Photo + certification + summit count + one direct quote
- **Route breakdowns:** Day-by-day altitude chart as a carousel
- **Myth vs. fact:** Common trekking misconceptions corrected with data
- **Field dispatches:** Short updates from actual departures (with trekker permission)
- **Preparation timelines:** "12 weeks out: this is what your training should look like"

### What to avoid on social:
- Motivational quotes with no substance ("The journey of a thousand miles…")
- Generic mountain sunset photos with no context
- Sales-first content ("Book now! Limited seats!")
- Over-filtered / over-produced imagery
- Posting about a trek without mentioning the guide leading it
